# Referee Report — Royal Society Open Science

**Referee 1 (uncertainty-quantification statistician)**

**Overall Recommendation:** `Major Revision`

RSOS is reviewed on soundness alone: valid methods, conclusions supported by the data, and complete/transparent reporting. Novelty and importance are explicitly not my remit. Against that bar, the underlying machinery is largely correct — the law-of-total-variance decomposition, the nonparametric mixture predictive interval, and the REML/Bayes secondary estimators are all implemented as described and I found no correctness error in them. The recommendation is Major Revision, not Minor, because (i) the headline policy-translation exercise (Table 4 / Table A13) is not reproducible as written — the text describes a microdata-calibrated Pareto tail that the committed artifacts do not use — and (ii) a recurring pattern of in-text numbers that disagree with the committed tables, plus a systematic quantity-count mislabeling, means several specific quantitative conclusions are not, as presented, supported by the committed data. All of these are fixable by re-syncing the prose to a single regenerated build and adding a few reporting elements; none requires new data collection.

---

## Major Comments

1. **The top-tax exercise (Table 4 and Table A13) is not reproducible as described; the committed artifacts used a fallback the text does not disclose.** The text states the Pareto tail parameter is microdata-calibrated at `a = 1.470`, gives a footnoted threshold of `$659,618` and tail mean `$2,062,980`, and reports implied top-rate medians "from `33.5%` for GPT-5.4 nano to `44.3%` for Gemini 3.1 Pro, with GPT-5.4 at `39.6%`" and "a `10.8` percentage-point cross-model spread" [paper.qmd:231], [paper.qmd:247]. The committed tables were generated on the **fallback** path with `a = 1.500`: the Table 4 note reads "fallback Pareto parameter a = 1.500" [toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md:1] and yields `32.8%` (GPT-5.4 nano) to `43.2%` (Gemini 3.1 Pro), GPT-5.4 at `38.8%`, a `10.4`pp spread. The build logic confirms this: `estimate_policyengine_top_tail_pareto_parameter()` returns `a = TOP_RATE_PARETO_A = 1.5` with `threshold=NaN`, `mean_above=NaN` whenever the PolicyEngine-US venv is absent or the subprocess fails [build_tables.py:1425], [build_tables.py:33], and the fallback note branch is what appears in the committed file [build_tables.py:262]. Worse, the committed Table A13 note internally contradicts the prose: it calls `a = 1.500` "the microdata estimate" [top-rate-robustness.md:1], whereas the text calls `1.470` the microdata estimate. I verified the arithmetic both ways: at `a=1.5, ḡ=0.6`, Gemini 3.1 Pro (ETI median `0.351`) maps to `0.4/(0.4+1.5·0.351)=43.2%`, matching the committed table; the text's `33.5%`/`39.6%` reproduce exactly only at `a=1.470`. So the two are genuinely different builds, and a reader running the public repo obtains the `a=1.500` numbers, not the ones in the text. Under RSOS "conclusions supported by data" and "complete reporting," this must be reconciled: either commit the microdata-derived tables so text and artifact agree, or make `a=1.500` the reproducible headline and clearly label the microdata variant. Whichever is chosen, the load-bearing spread ("`10.8` percentage-point") must match the committed table.

2. **The predictive-uncertainty ranking is a headline object but conflates two components with different provenance, one of which is generated under non-comparable sampling regimes.** Tables 1-2 and Appendix A2 rank models by pooled 90% width, and the text draws substantive conclusions from that ordering ("Some models behave as if the relevant literature is tight and settled; others behave as if the same quantities are wide-open or as if their own answers are unstable across repeated runs" [paper.qmd:269]). But the pooled predictive spread is `Var = E[within-run var] + Var[run means]` (correctly implemented at [aggregate.py:50]), and the two terms have different sources: the within-run term is *stated* uncertainty (the model's own p05-p95), whereas the between-run term is *sampling* variability. The between-run term is not comparable across models here, because the sampling regime is heterogeneous by design: 14 models run at `temperature = 1.0`, but Claude Fable 5 / Opus 4.8 / Sonnet 5 reject sampling parameters and vary only under provider-default (unstated, non-settable) sampling, with heterogeneous reasoning configs (always-on / adaptive / off) [paper.qmd:143]; OpenAI models additionally draw batched `n=8`. Notably, Claude Fable 5 — a no-sampling-parameter, always-on-reasoning model — is ranked the *tightest* model in A2 (avg pooled rank `5.15`) [pooling-robustness-appendix.md:5], and the design cannot distinguish whether that tightness is a content property or an artifact of a low effective sampling temperature. The code already computes `within_run_sd`, `between_run_sd`, and `total_sd` [aggregate.py:85] but the paper never surfaces the decomposition. In fairness, the *headline* pooled width is dominated by the within-run stated quantiles (the quantity-disagreement table shows cross-model spread well below mean width throughout), which mitigates the concern — but the paper should demonstrate this rather than leave the reader to assume it. **Request:** report the within-/between-run variance split (at least averaged over each subpanel), show the between-run share is small, and explicitly caveat the predictive-uncertainty ranking for the three no-sampling-parameter models.

3. **Several appendix diagnostics are computed over 13 quantities but labeled "nine-quantity," and one downstream claim is consequently wrong.** The code's "canonical" bucket is *everything that is not simulation, legacy, or the cap-gains sibling* [build_tables.py:1789], which is 13 quantities (the 9 canonical elasticities plus coefficient of relative risk aversion, capital share in production, annual discount factor, and TFP persistence). The disagreement table (A6) accordingly has 13 rows [quantity-disagreement.md:1], yet the text calls it "the canonical nine-quantity panel" [paper.qmd:334], and A1/A2/A4 are likewise built from the 13-quantity `canonical_rows` while their notes say "nine-quantity." This mislabeling has a concrete consequence: the text asserts "the largest spread-to-width ratio occurring for the capital gains realizations elasticity" [paper.qmd:217], but the committed table shows the largest ratio is TFP persistence (`0.653`), then IES (`0.548`), with capital gains third (`0.516`); even restricting to the intended nine, IES (`0.548`) exceeds capital gains (`0.516`). The stability table compounds this: its "Cells compared" is `221` for the 5- and 10-run rows (= 17 models × 13 quantities) but `153` for the 15-run row (= 17 × 9), because the 15-run row is counted over `LABOR_TAX | MACRO_TRADE` only [build_tables.py:1009], [stability-appendix.md:5]. So the stability medians are not even computed over a fixed cell set across rows. **Request:** relabel A1/A2/A4/A6 with the true quantity count, make the cell set consistent, and correct the capital-gains claim.

4. **A recurring pattern of in-text numbers that do not match the committed artifacts (prose appears written against an earlier build).** Beyond items 1 and 3: (a) "The largest rank spread is `1.42` positions for `Claude Sonnet 4.6`" [paper.qmd:300] — the committed table's maximum is `1.85` (Claude Sonnet 4.6); `1.42` is Grok 4.1 Fast [pooling-robustness-appendix.md:13]. (b) "The largest average rank change is `1.54` positions for `Claude Opus 4.7`" [paper.qmd:316] — the committed table's maximum |shift| is `1.88` (Claude Sonnet 4.6); Opus 4.7 is `1.77` [quantile-rule-appendix.md:7]. (c) The stability medians in the text — "first 10 runs ... median of `0.001` ... width ... `0.009`; ... first 5 runs ... `0.003` ... `0.022`" [paper.qmd:110] — do not match the committed table (`0.002`/`0.006` at 10 runs; `0.003`/`0.02` at 5 runs) [stability-appendix.md:5]. (d) The A13 shift ranges ("up by `4.5` to `4.8`" for a→1.3; "down by `5.0` to `5.7`" for a→1.7 [paper.qmd:249]) are the `a=1.470`-baseline deltas; the committed `a=1.500`-baseline table gives roughly `+5.2` to `+5.6` and `−4.3` to `−4.9`. Individually these are small, but collectively they mean the manuscript's specific numbers are not, as submitted, reproducible from the committed tables. **Request:** regenerate every table from one committed build and reconcile each in-text figure to it.

5. **The capital-gains implied-τ audit (A9) reports a median-and-interval summary of a heavy-tailed ratio distribution with a pole, and treats non-identified rows as if they were informative.** The inversion `τ = −ρ/(1−ρ)` with `ρ = ε_τ/ε_{1−τ}` has a pole at `ρ=1` and near-poles wherever a bootstrapped `ε_{1−τ}` draw is small; the bootstrap keeps all finite draws and drops only exact singularities [build_tables.py:1177]. For the two rows where an elasticity centers near zero, the resulting 90% interval spans the pole and is essentially uninformative: GPT-5.4 nano reports `0.857 [-3.592, 5.000]` and Gemini 3.5 Flash `0.452 [-0.889, 3.250]` [cap-gains-convention-audit.md:5], [cap-gains-convention-audit.md:18]. A median of such a distribution is not a stable central summary. More seriously, GPT-5.4 nano is flagged "both negative" — i.e. the identity's premise (opposite signs) fails — yet it is still assigned a numeric τ (`0.857`) and a band ("plausible sign, outside bands"), and Gemini 3.5 Flash is counted as an "ordinary-income-rate consistent" cluster member despite an interval of `[-0.889, 3.250]`. This inflates the audit's central claim that "most sign-consistent models cluster near `τ ≈ 0.5`" [paper.qmd:360]. The substantive finding is fine for the away-from-pole, sign-consistent rows (12 of 17), but the reporting should not present pole-straddling medians as if they were precise. **Request:** suppress implied-τ for rows that are not sign-consistent (as is already done for non-finite cases), report the share of bootstrap draws falling in `(0,1)`, and mark pole-straddling rows as uninformative rather than band-consistent.

6. **`R = 15` is defended only by a within-sample prefix-stability check, which is not a sampling-error statement for the headline ranking.** The prefix check (A1) shows the pooled center/width move little when runs 11-15 are added [paper.qmd:110]; the paper honestly concedes this "does not prove convergence." But the predictive-uncertainty ranking depends on per-cell between-run variance estimated from 15 draws, and a variance estimate from 15 points has a large relative standard error (~35-40% under normality). A rank over 17 models built on such noisy per-cell variances could reshuffle materially under resampling, and neither A2 (estimator choice) nor A4 (quantile rule) addresses sampling error. **Request:** add a Monte Carlo standard error for the pooled center and the pooled 90% width (e.g. resample the 15 runs within each cell) and a rank-stability diagnostic (how often the reported width ordering is preserved across resamples). This is the single most useful addition for substantiating that the ranking is signal rather than `R=15` noise.

## Minor Comments

- **Residual Bayesian/"belief" language for an object that does not support it.** The prose is otherwise disciplined about the estimand, but a few load-bearing places still use "elicited beliefs" [paper.qmd:392], "single tau prior" [paper.qmd:257], and "joint posterior" [paper.qmd:362]. The measured object is a bootstrap over elicited point estimates, not a posterior; prefer "jointly consistent with a single τ" and "elicited responses."

- **The reported center and the reported interval come from two different elicited fields.** The pooled point estimate is the mean of run-level `point_estimate`s [paper.qmd:106], whereas the intervals — and the ETI feeding Table 4 — use the mixture-of-quantiles median [build_tables.py:615]. These differ: from the committed comparison CSV, GPT-5.4 nano ETI center = `0.552` but toy-table median = `0.546`; Claude Sonnet 5 center = `0.473` (the value quoted at [paper.qmd:215]) but toy-table median = `0.471`. The choice is disclosed, but using two different central estimators for the same quantity across tables invites confusion; consider reporting the mixture median alongside the mean center, or noting the gap.

- **`between_run_var` uses the population variance** (`pvariance`, dividing by `R` not `R−1`) [aggregate.py:50], a mild downward bias at `R=15`. Low stakes because the headline interval is the nonparametric mixture rather than a Gaussian built from `total_sd`, but worth a one-line note.

- **Per-quantity support bounds are load-bearing but never tabulated.** The bounds `L_k, U_k` drive the tail extrapolation, the bounded-logit / one-sided-log transform used by REML/Bayes [aggregate.py:453], and the ETI truncation. They should appear in an appendix registry table so the reconstruction is fully specified.

- **The tail-extrapolation rule is thin and heterogeneous in effect.** Unbounded quantities get support endpoints only `0.25 ×` the inter-quantile gap beyond q05/q95 [distributions.py:197], which forces a thin tail and biases `within_run_variance` (hence REML/Bayes and `total_sd`) downward for models that imply heavy tails. Please note that the headline 5-95 mixture interval is largely *insensitive* to this (its endpoints sit at/near the elicited q05/q95 knots) — this is a genuine robustness strength worth stating — while the SD-based and REML/Bayes summaries are more exposed.

- **"The replacement does not select on elicited values" is asserted, not shown** [paper.qmd:160]. Two of the listed failure modes (gpt-5.5 exhausting the completion budget on reasoning; Sonnet 5 exceeding a 32k output cap) could plausibly correlate with harder/more-uncertain cells, and if re-draws used a larger token budget than the first attempt, that is a within-cell protocol change. A one-line comparison of replaced vs non-replaced cell centers/widths, or a softer claim, would close this.

- **Armington clarified intervals hit the support floor at exactly `0` for Gemini 3 Flash and Gemini 3.1 Pro** [armington-clarify-delta.md:15], [armington-clarify-delta.md:17], which indicates support clamping rather than an elicited lower bound; worth a note so readers do not read `0` as a stated quantile.

## Strengths

1. **The pooling machinery is correct and the headline interval is appropriately nonparametric.** The law-of-total-variance decomposition [aggregate.py:50] and the equal-weight mixture predictive interval [aggregate.py:573] are implemented as described, and the interval makes no normality assumption — it is the `[F⁻¹(0.05), F⁻¹(0.95)]` of the run-level mixture, with endpoints anchored at the elicited quantiles.

2. **The estimand is now defined carefully** as a prompt-and-sampling-conditioned response distribution, and "belief" language is largely purged from the prose. This is the right conceptual object for what is actually measured, and the paper does not overclaim latent priors.

3. **The REML and Bayesian hierarchical estimators are correctly specified.** The profiled REML objective [aggregate.py:303] and the collapsed-conjugate grid posterior [aggregate.py:322] are standard and sound, and A2 shows the three constructions give coherent rankings — a legitimate robustness result.

4. **The sign-convention clarifier is symmetric and its residual ordering-anchoring is candidly disclosed** [paper.qmd:63], and the capital-gains audit correctly bootstraps the implied-τ distribution while explicitly acknowledging the Jensen/nonlinearity issue — the right instinct even though the pole handling (Major 5) needs work.

## Concrete Requests for Revision

1. Regenerate **all** tables from a single committed build and reconcile every in-text number to it. If the public reproduction path cannot reach PolicyEngine microdata, report the reproducible fallback (`a=1.500`) as the headline and clearly mark the microdata `a=1.470` variant; otherwise commit the microdata-derived tables so text and artifacts agree. Fix the internal contradiction in the A13 note that calls `1.500` "the microdata estimate."

2. Add a within-/between-run variance decomposition (per model, averaged over each subpanel), demonstrate the between-run share is small, and add a one-line-per-model table of sampling temperature and reasoning configuration. Explicitly caveat the predictive-uncertainty ranking for Claude Fable 5 / Opus 4.8 / Sonnet 5, whose between-run variation is provider-default and non-settable.

3. Relabel A1/A2/A4/A6 with the actual quantity count (13, not 9), fix the stability "cells compared" so the cell set is fixed across prefixes, and correct the "capital gains has the largest spread-to-width ratio" claim (it is IES among the nine, TFP persistence among the thirteen).

4. Add a resampling-based Monte Carlo standard error for the pooled center and the pooled 90% width, and a rank-stability diagnostic for the predictive-uncertainty ordering, to substantiate `R=15` for the headline ranking.

5. In A9, suppress implied-τ for non-sign-consistent rows, report the share of bootstrap draws in `(0,1)`, and flag pole-straddling rows (GPT-5.4 nano, Gemini 3.5 Flash) as uninformative rather than band-consistent.

6. Report per-quantity support bounds `L_k, U_k` in an appendix, and add one sentence noting the headline 5-95 interval is insensitive to the tail-extrapolation rule while the SD-based / REML / Bayes summaries are more sensitive.
